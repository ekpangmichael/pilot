Chapter 01 / Mindset shift

From engineers to operators.

Your co-founders should still use their engineering skills — but on operations, data, tracking, reporting, creator quality and campaign execution, not on building a full app. The real risk now is not lack of software. The real risk is no repeat paying businesses.

Before Engineer
For 90 days Operator

Rebuild the backend platform.

Add features nobody has paid for.

Hide behind code.

The brutal truth

A technical co-founder who helps you close, run, track and report campaigns for paying customers is more valuable right now than one who quietly builds features nobody has paid for.

Chapter 02 / The role split

Three founders. Three engines.

Each role owns one business engine. Nobody covers more than one. This is the only way to stop all three of you drifting into general-purpose busywork that doesn't move the company forward.

Role 01 · You

CEO.

Sales · Strategy
  • Close paying SMEs.
  • Pick the niche. Set the menu. Defend pricing.
  • Lead outreach & close paid pilots.
  • Control the campaign promise.
  • Own partnerships and repeat buyers.
Role 02 · Mobile co-founder

Creator Lead.

Creators · Campaigns
  • Recruit and vet 30–50 creators.
  • Run creator tiers & reliability scoring.
  • Own content review and revision flow.
  • Coordinate posting and proof.
  • Build the reliable creator supply system.
Role 03 · Backend co-founder

Data Lead.

Tracking · Payouts · Reports
  • Run the single campaign tracker.
  • Maintain the creator database.
  • Tracking, fraud checks, payout proof.
  • Deliver every report within 72 hours.
  • Build internal tooling — never the product.
Why this works: creators are mobile-first — they live on WhatsApp, TikTok, Instagram. A mobile engineer naturally understands user flow, friction and onboarding. Backend engineers naturally understand structured data, tracking and reporting. Both apply that instinct to operations first — not to a product nobody has paid for yet.
Chapter 03 / Role 01 · You

CEO — find the buyer.

Focus on the thing that can kill the company fastest: can TrendStack get Nigerian SMEs to pay repeatedly? Don't spend most of your time managing creators. Spend it finding out whether businesses will pay.

A · NICHE

Pick the first vertical

Beauty, fashion, food, restaurants or startups. Pick one. Defend it. Don't dilute the offer across all five.

B · PRICING

Packages & price

Set the menu. Hold the line on price. Refuse what doesn't fit. Decide what TrendStack will and will not offer.

C · OUTREACH

Lead the sale

Speak to SME owners daily. Send proposals fast. Close paid pilots. This is the only activity that proves the company.

D · PROMISE

Own the campaign promise

Control what TrendStack will deliver. Protect the brand from bad campaigns. Reject creators and briefs that don't fit.

E · REVIEW

Read every result

Sit with the business on every campaign report. Decide what's next. Close the loop yourself — don't delegate the verdict.

F · REPEAT

Drive repeat business

Build case studies. Push for repeat bookings. Decide which niche actually works once you have 5 paid campaigns of evidence.

Chapter 04 / Role 02 · Mobile co-founder

Creator & Campaign Lead.

Design the creator experience as if it were an app — but execute it through WhatsApp, Forms, Sheets and Drive. The job is not "bring influencers." The job is to build a reliable creator supply system. No app yet.

A · RECRUIT

The vetted bench

30–50 creators in 21 days. Capture niche, follower count, average views, audience, sample work, rate, reliability and fraud risk for each.

B · SCORE

Tier the creators

New · Verified · Trusted. Small fixed-fee gigs only for new. Better campaigns and small bonuses unlocked at Trusted.

C · ONBOARD

Application & rules

Application form, welcome message, participation rules, content rules, payment expectations, disclosure reminders.

D · REVIEW

The 9-step loop

Brief → confirm → draft → review → approve / revise → post → proof → analytics → pay. Same loop, every campaign.

E · CHANNELS

Disciplined comms

One announcement channel. One campaign chat. One form. One tracker. Never one chaotic group where everyone talks.

F · IMPROVE

Drop & promote

Track dropout. Remove unreliable creators. Promote consistent ones into the Trusted tier. Tighten the pool every cycle.

Chapter 05 / Role 03 · Backend co-founder

Data, tracking, payouts & reports.

Don't build the full backend yet. Build the manual data infrastructure that lets campaigns run without confusion. Every campaign tracked. Every creator logged. Every naira accounted for. Every result reportable. Every fraud signal noticed.

A · BUILD

Campaign tracker

One Sheet / Airtable. Business, package, budget, creators, status, payment, tracking link, results — all in one place.

B · STRUCTURE

Creator database

Creator ID, niche, follower count, engagement, history, on-time score, fraud risk, tier, payment history. Future schema, today.

C · TRACK

Per-campaign signal

UTMs, Bitly, WhatsApp links, coupon codes, screenshots, manual enquiry logs. Credible — not perfect. Just enough to show ROI.

D · DETECT

Fraud & quality rules

View spikes, generic comments, audience mismatch, post deletion, manipulated screenshots. Simple rules + manual review. No AI.

E · PAY

Payout ledger

Fee, approval, bonus, due date, status, posting proof, screenshot proof, hold reason. Margin per campaign. No casual payments via random WhatsApp instruction.

F · REPORT

72-hour report

Goal, posts, views, link clicks, coupon usage, orders / leads, best creator, next steps. One reusable template, fraud checklist, payout checklist.

Boundaries

The next 90 days: nothing else.

Engineering instinct will pull your co-founders back toward building. Resist it. Here is the line — what's forbidden, what's done by hand, and the small set of tools that are allowed.

Forbidden

Do not build.

  • A new mobile app.
  • The old campaign marketplace, rebuilt.
  • Complex dashboards or matching.
  • Automated payments or AI fraud detection.
  • Hundreds of creators recruited at once.
  • Anything that hides from the sales conversation.
Manual ops

Do by hand.

  • Creator vetting, onboarding, communication.
  • Brief writing, submission review, revisions.
  • Posting confirmation & screenshot proof.
  • Tracking via UTM, Bitly, coupons, manual logs.
  • Payout approval and ledger entry.
  • Campaign reports in Docs / Slides.
Lightweight tools

Allowed now.

  • Landing page for SME packages.
  • Creator application & business inquiry forms.
  • Campaign tracker & creator database in Sheets.
  • Payout ledger & report template.
  • Link tracking & coupon setup.
  • Internal campaign status dashboard.
Two rules

If it helps run paid campaigns this week, build it. If it is for a future scalable platform, delay it. Aim for 80% operations · 20% lightweight tools.

Chapter 06 / Cadence

90 days. Weekly. Then decide.

Run on a tight rhythm so nobody drifts. Four phases. One founder meeting a week. One clear bar to clear before any software is allowed back.

01
Weeks 1–2 · Setup

Build the manual rails.

Everyone builds in parallel. Nothing is being sold yet — but every piece of infrastructure for the first sale gets laid down.

  • You: finalize offer, pricing, prospect list. Start sales calls.
  • Creator Lead: recruit and vet 30–50 creators. Build the scoring sheet.
  • Data Lead: build the campaign tracker, creator database, payout ledger, report template.
02
Weeks 3–6 · First sales

Close and deliver.

First paid pilots are live. Operations get stress-tested. Everyone touches a real campaign.

  • You: close paid pilots. Daily SME conversations. Defend pricing.
  • Creator Lead: match creators to campaigns. Review submissions. Track reliability.
  • Data Lead: track progress. Monitor links and codes. Manage payouts. Prepare reports.
03
Weeks 7–10 · Repeat & refine

Push toward repeat.

Now we're optimizing. Case studies emerge. Bad creators get dropped. The pool tightens. The story sharpens.

  • You: push for repeat campaigns. Build case studies. Decide which niche wins.
  • Creator Lead: improve the pool. Remove unreliable creators. Create the Trusted tier.
  • Data Lead: improve fraud checks. Raise report quality. Analyze campaign margin.
04
Weeks 11–13 · Decide

Verdict on the model.

All three of you sit down with the data. Continue, narrow, or change. Identify the top repeated pains worth automating.

  • Review results — paid campaigns, repeat customers, margin, creator quality.
  • Decide whether to continue the manual model.
  • Identify what, if anything, should become software.
  • List the top repeated manual pains.

Weekly meeting

Ten questions. One hour.

One founder meeting a week. Ask only these ten questions. Skip everything else. It keeps the team focused on business proof — not activity.

Founders standup

Run this top to bottom. Don't drift.

  1. How many businesses did we speak to?
  2. How many proposals did we send?
  3. How many paid campaigns did we close?
  4. Which creators are campaign-ready?
  5. Which creators failed or behaved badly?
  6. Which campaigns are currently active?
  7. Which reports are due?
  8. Did we make margin?
  9. Did any business ask to repeat?
  10. What manual process is painful enough to automate?
Return clause

When software is allowed back.

Build software only when you can say: "We have done this manually 10–20 times, and the same workflow keeps repeating." Until then, software is premature.

Green · build

10+ paid campaigns completed. 3+ repeat customers. A known winning segment. A stable workflow. A known report format.

Decision Start building the smallest piece of software that automates the most painful manual step. Nothing more.
Yellow · hold

Paid campaigns running, but margin or repeat is weak. Reports inconsistent. Some creators unreliable.

Decision Don't build yet. Fix pricing, tighten the bench, raise report quality. Re-check at 30 days.
Red · do not build

Interest without payment. Chaotic campaigns. No repeat. Margin unclear. Founders drifting.

Decision Stop. Re-scope the offer or the target customer. Software does not solve a missing buyer.

Earn the right to be engineers again.

We are not abandoning software — we are discovering what software is worth building. The manual phase is research, sales validation, operational design, and product discovery. Run the playbook. Deliver strong reports. Find the repeat buyer. Then decide what software is actually needed.
