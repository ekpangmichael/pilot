Chapter 01 / Mindset shift

From engineers to operators.

We still use our engineering skills — but on operations, data, tracking, reporting, creator quality and campaign execution. Not on building an app. The risk right now isn't lack of software. The risk is no repeat paying businesses.

Before Engineer
For 90 days Operator
Build a mobile app.
Build the creator-side experience by hand.
Rebuild the backend platform.
Run operations in Sheets, Forms and Drive.
Add features nobody has paid for.
Track every paid campaign end to end.
Hide behind code.
Talk to creators, businesses and reports.
The brutal truth

Right now, a technical co-founder who helps us close, run, track and report campaigns for paying customers is more valuable than one quietly building features nobody has paid for.

Chapter 02 / The role split

Three founders. Three engines.

How we split the work. Each of us owns one engine. Nobody covers more than one. This is the only way to stop us drifting into general-purpose busywork.

Role 01 · Michael (me)

CEO.

Sales · Strategy
  • Close paying SMEs.
  • Pick the niche, set the menu, defend pricing.
  • Lead outreach and close paid pilots.
  • Control the campaign promise.
  • Own partnerships and repeat buyers.
Role 02 · Samuel

Creator Lead.

Creators · Campaigns
  • Recruit and vet 30–50 creators.
  • Run creator tiers and reliability scoring.
  • Own content review and revision flow.
  • Coordinate posting and proof.
  • Build the reliable creator supply system.
Role 03 · Daniel

Data Lead.

Tracking · Payouts · Reports
  • Run the single campaign tracker.
  • Maintain the creator database.
  • Own tracking, fraud checks, payout proof.
  • Deliver every report within 72 hours.
  • Build internal tooling — never the product.
Why this split: creators are mobile-first, so the creator side fits Samuel's instinct for flow and onboarding. The operating system fits Daniel's instinct for structured data, tracking and reporting. Both apply that instinct to operations first — not to a product nobody has paid for yet.
Chapter 03 / Role 01 · Me

CEO — find the buyer.

My focus is the thing that can kill us fastest: can we get Nigerian SMEs to pay us repeatedly? I don't manage creators — that's Samuel's seat. I spend my time finding out whether businesses will actually pay.

A · NICHE

Pick the vertical

Beauty, fashion, food, restaurants or startups. One. Defend it. No diluting across all five.

B · PRICING

Packages & price

Set the menu. Hold the line on price. Decide what we will and will not offer.

C · OUTREACH

Lead the sale

Speak to SME owners daily. Send proposals fast. Close paid pilots. The only activity that proves us.

D · PROMISE

Own the campaign promise

Control what we deliver. Protect us from bad campaigns. Reject creators and briefs that don't fit.

E · REVIEW

Read every result

Sit with the business on every campaign report. Decide what's next. Close the loop myself — no delegation on the verdict.

F · REPEAT

Drive repeat business

Build case studies. Push for repeat bookings. Decide which niche actually works after 5 paid campaigns of evidence.

Chapter 04 / Role 02 · Samuel

Creator & Campaign Lead.

Samuel — design the creator experience like a mobile app, but execute it through WhatsApp, Forms, Sheets and Drive. The job isn't "bring influencers." It's to build a reliable creator supply system. No actual app yet.

A · RECRUIT

Build the vetted bench

30–50 creators in 21 days. For each: niche, follower count, average views, audience, sample work, rate, reliability, fraud risk.

B · SCORE

Tier them

New · Verified · Trusted. New creators get fixed-fee gigs only. Better campaigns and bonuses unlock at Trusted.

C · ONBOARD

Set the rules

Application form, welcome message, participation rules, content rules, payment expectations, disclosure reminders.

D · REVIEW

Run the 9-step loop

Brief → confirm → draft → review → approve / revise → post → proof → analytics → pay. Same loop, every campaign.

E · CHANNELS

Disciplined comms

One announcement channel. One campaign chat. One form. One tracker. No chaotic group where everyone talks.

F · IMPROVE

Drop & promote

Track dropout. Remove unreliable creators. Promote consistent ones into Trusted. Tighten the pool every cycle.

Chapter 05 / Role 03 · Daniel

Data, tracking, payouts & reports.

Daniel — not the full backend yet. Build the manual data infrastructure that lets us run campaigns without confusion. Every campaign tracked. Every creator logged. Every naira accounted for. Every report shipped. Every fraud signal seen.

A · BUILD

Campaign tracker

One Sheet / Airtable. Business, package, budget, creators, status, payment, tracking link, results. The single source of truth.

B · STRUCTURE

Creator database

Creator ID, niche, follower count, engagement, history, on-time score, fraud risk, tier, payment history. Future schema today.

C · TRACK

Per-campaign signal

UTMs, Bitly, WhatsApp links, coupon codes, screenshots, manual enquiry logs. Credible — not perfect. Enough to show ROI.

D · DETECT

Fraud rules

View spikes, generic comments, audience mismatch, post deletion, fake screenshots. Simple rules + manual review. No AI yet.

E · PAY

Payout ledger

Fee, approval, bonus, due date, status, posting proof, hold reason. Margin per campaign. Every payout through the ledger — no casual WhatsApp payments.

F · REPORT

72-hour report

Goal, posts, views, link clicks, coupon usage, orders / leads, best creator, next steps. One reusable template, fraud checklist, payout checklist.

Boundaries

The next 90 days: nothing else.

Our engineering instinct will pull us back to building. We resist it. Here's the line — what's forbidden, what we do by hand, and the small set of tools that are allowed.

Forbidden

Do not build.

  • A new mobile app.
  • The old campaign marketplace, rebuilt.
  • Complex dashboards or matching.
  • Automated payments or AI fraud detection.
  • Hundreds of creators recruited at once.
  • Anything that hides from the sales conversation.
Manual ops

Do by hand.

  • Creator vetting, onboarding, communication.
  • Brief writing, submission review, revisions.
  • Posting confirmation & screenshot proof.
  • Tracking via UTM, Bitly, coupons, manual logs.
  • Payout approval and ledger entry.
  • Campaign reports in Docs / Slides.
Lightweight tools

Allowed now.

  • Landing page for SME packages.
  • Creator application & business inquiry forms.
  • Campaign tracker & creator database in Sheets.
  • Payout ledger & report template.
  • Link tracking & coupon setup.
  • Internal campaign status dashboard.
Two rules

If it helps run paid campaigns this week, build it. If it's for a future scalable platform, delay it. Aim for 80% operations · 20% lightweight tools.

Chapter 06 / Cadence

90 days. Weekly. Then decide.

Tight rhythm so none of us drifts. Four phases. One founders' meeting a week. One clear bar before software is allowed back.

01
Weeks 1–2 · Setup

Build the manual rails.

We build in parallel. Nothing sold yet — but the infrastructure for the first sale gets laid down.

  • Me: finalize offer, pricing, prospect list. Start sales calls.
  • Samuel: recruit and vet 30–50 creators. Build the scoring sheet.
  • Daniel: campaign tracker, creator database, payout ledger, report template.
02
Weeks 3–6 · First sales

Close and deliver.

First paid pilots go live. Operations get stress-tested. All three of us touch a real campaign.

  • Me: close paid pilots. Daily SME conversations. Defend pricing.
  • Samuel: match creators to campaigns. Review submissions. Track reliability.
  • Daniel: track progress. Monitor links and codes. Manage payouts. Prepare reports.
03
Weeks 7–10 · Repeat & refine

Push toward repeat.

We optimize. Case studies emerge. Bad creators get dropped. The pool tightens. The story sharpens.

  • Me: push for repeat campaigns. Build case studies. Decide which niche wins.
  • Samuel: improve the pool. Remove unreliable creators. Build the Trusted tier.
  • Daniel: improve fraud checks. Raise report quality. Analyze margin.
04
Weeks 11–13 · Decide

The verdict.

The three of us sit down with the data. Continue, narrow, or change. We list the top repeated pains worth automating.

  • Review results — paid campaigns, repeat customers, margin, creator quality.
  • Decide whether to continue the manual model.
  • Identify what, if anything, should become software.
  • List the top repeated manual pains.

Weekly meeting

Ten questions. One hour.

One founders' meeting a week. We ask only these ten and skip everything else. Keeps us focused on business proof, not activity.

Founders standup

Top to bottom. No drifting.

  1. How many businesses did we speak to?
  2. How many proposals did we send?
  3. How many paid campaigns did we close?
  4. Which creators are campaign-ready?
  5. Which creators failed or behaved badly?
  6. Which campaigns are currently active?
  7. Which reports are due?
  8. Did we make margin?
  9. Did any business ask to repeat?
  10. What manual process is painful enough to automate?
Return clause

When software is allowed back.

We go back to building only when we can honestly say: "We've done this manually 10–20 times, and the same workflow keeps repeating." Until then, software is premature.

Green · build

10+ paid campaigns completed. 3+ repeat customers. A known winning segment. A stable workflow. A known report format.

Decision Build the smallest piece of software that automates the most painful manual step. Nothing more.
Yellow · hold

Paid campaigns running but margin or repeat is weak. Reports inconsistent. Some creators unreliable.

Decision Don't build yet. Fix pricing, tighten the bench, raise report quality. Re-check at 30 days.
Red · do not build

Interest without payment. Chaotic campaigns. No repeat. Margin unclear. Founders drifting.

Decision Stop. Re-scope the offer or the target customer. Software doesn't solve a missing buyer.

We earn the right to be engineers again.

We are not abandoning software — we are discovering what's worth building. The manual phase is our research, sales validation, operational design and product discovery rolled into one. Run the playbook. Deliver strong reports. Find the repeat buyer. Then we decide what software is actually needed.
